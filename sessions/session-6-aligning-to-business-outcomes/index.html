Measure What Matters

John Doerr's field guide to OKRs, drawn from Intel, Google, and dozens of companies in between

Doerr learned OKRs from Andy Grove at Intel and carried them to Google in 1999, when Larry Page and Sergey Brin were running a company of 40 people out of a garage. The book argues that goals fail not from lack of effort but from lack of clarity — teams work hard on the wrong things because no one wrote down, in measurable terms, what winning actually looks like.

The Lean Startup

Eric Ries's method for testing ideas before betting the roadmap on them

Ries argues that most startup and product failure isn't a failure of execution — it's a failure to validate the idea before pouring resources into it. The antidote is treating every product decision as a hypothesis, testing it with the smallest possible experiment, and measuring whether customers actually behave differently — not just whether the feature shipped.

Core Themes
→  The Build-Measure-Learn feedback loop
→  Minimum Viable Product (MVP) as a learning tool, not a lesser product
→  Validated learning over feature delivery
→  Innovation accounting: measuring progress under uncertainty

The OKR Framework

The what, the how, and the discipline that makes goals stick

🎯

Objectives & Key Results

Two questions every goal has to answer

O

Objective — what, and why it matters

Significant, concrete, and action-oriented. A good Objective is memorable enough to repeat from memory and inspiring enough that people want to work toward it — not a KPI dressed up in aspirational language.

KR

Key Result — how we'll know we got there

Specific, time-bound, and measurable. If a Key Result can be satisfied by "we worked on it," it's a task, not a Key Result. It should be verifiable with a number, not a feeling.

"Ideas are easy. Execution is everything." — Larry Page, foreword to Measure What Matters

⚡

The Four OKR Superpowers

What OKRs do that a spreadsheet of goals doesn't

Focus & Commit

A handful of priorities, stated clearly, beats a long list everyone half-remembers. Saying yes to an OKR means saying no to everything that doesn't serve it.

Align & Connect

Team OKRs are visibly linked to company OKRs. Everyone can trace their weekly work up to a business result, not just a manager's ask.

Track for Accountability

OKRs are graded, not just written. Regular check-ins surface whether a Key Result is on track, at risk, or dead — before the quarter ends.

Stretch for Amazing

Aspirational OKRs are meant to be uncomfortable. A team that hits 100% of every OKR every quarter isn't excelling — it's sandbagging.

⚖️

Committed vs. Aspirational Goals

Not every OKR should be graded the same way

Committed Goals

  • Tied to metrics the business is publicly counting on — revenue, launch dates, compliance deadlines
  • Resourced and scheduled to be fully achieved
  • Missing one is a real problem worth a retro, not a shrug
  • Should be graded near 1.0 — if it's regularly missed, it wasn't a committed goal, it was a stretch goal mislabeled

Aspirational Goals

  • Reflect a bigger, riskier ambition than current resources guarantee
  • Meant to expand what the team believes is possible
  • A 0.7 average score is healthy — it means the bar was set high enough to matter
  • Failure here is data, not a performance issue — that's the point of setting a stretch goal

Grading an aspirational goal like a committed one just teaches people to stop setting aspirational goals.

💬

CFRs: The Human System Behind the Numbers

OKRs set the destination; CFRs are the conversations that get you there

Conversations

  • Authentic, ongoing exchange between manager and report
  • Focused on performance, not just a annual review script
  • Replaces the once-a-year surprise with continuous calibration

Feedback

  • Bidirectional, peer-to-peer, not just top-down
  • Specific and tied to an observable behavior or result
  • Given close to the moment it's earned, not batched for later

Recognition

  • Calls out contributions that moved a Key Result forward
  • Visible to the team, not private and easy to miss
  • Reinforces the behaviors you actually want repeated
Lean Startup Thinking

Before you commit a quarter of engineering to it, test whether it's worth building

🔁

Build — Measure — Learn

The core loop for turning assumptions into validated learning

Build

  • Turn the riskiest assumption into the smallest testable thing
  • An MVP is a learning tool, not a stripped-down product
  • Speed matters more than polish at this stage

Measure

  • Instrument the experiment before you ship it, not after
  • Choose actionable metrics tied to a real customer behavior
  • Decide in advance what result would prove you wrong

Learn

  • Validated learning is the real unit of progress — not story points shipped
  • Feed what you learned back into the next hypothesis
  • Be honest about disconfirming evidence — that's the whole point

"The only way to win is to learn faster than anyone else." — Eric Ries

🧭

Pivot or Persevere

A structured decision, made on a cadence — not a crisis reaction

Signs You Should Pivot

  • Engagement or conversion metrics are flat despite real iteration
  • Customers use the workaround, not the feature you built
  • The team's explanations for "why it's not working yet" keep changing
  • You're improving a metric that was never tied to the business outcome

Signs You Should Persevere

  • Actionable metrics are moving, even if slowly
  • Customers who try it are retained or come back unprompted
  • The hypothesis hasn't actually been tested yet — you shipped, but didn't measure
  • Leading indicators are healthy even though lagging ones aren't yet
Outcomes Over Output

Applying both books to engineering leadership specifically

🚦

Vanity Metrics vs. Actionable Metrics

One makes a slide look good. The other changes a decision.

Vanity Metrics

  • Lines of code written, tickets closed, story points burned
  • Cumulative totals that only ever go up
  • Number of features shipped, regardless of adoption
  • Feels like progress but doesn't tell you what to do differently

Actionable Metrics

  • p95 latency, error rate, cost per transaction
  • Activation rate, retention curve, feature adoption
  • Cohort-based, so you can see cause and effect
  • Tells you specifically what to build or fix next

If a metric can't change your next decision, it's decoration, not data.

A Good vs. Bad Engineering OKR

The same initiative, written two ways

Output, Disguised as a Goal
Migrate the platform to Kubernetes
KR Migrate service A to the new cluster
KR Migrate service B to the new cluster
KR Decommission the old infrastructure
Outcome, Tied to the Business
Delight customers with a fast, reliable product experience
KR Reduce p95 page load time from 3.2s to under 1s
KR Increase infrastructure NPS from 42 to 55
KR Cut customer-reported performance incidents by 50%

The migration might be exactly how you hit the second version's Key Results — but written as tasks, it can "succeed" even if customers never notice. Written as outcomes, the team has to prove the work mattered.

Putting It Into Practice

What engineering managers and directors can do this quarter

🗓

30 / 60 / 90 Day Leader Actions

A phased plan for making OKRs and validated learning part of how the team operates

30 Days

  • Draft next quarter's team OKRs and trace each Key Result up to a company Objective
  • Audit your current dashboards for vanity vs. actionable metrics
  • Rewrite one existing "task list" OKR as an outcome-based one
  • Identify the riskiest unvalidated assumption on the roadmap

60 Days

  • Launch a weekly OKR check-in ritual with confidence scoring
  • Ship one MVP experiment before committing full engineering effort
  • Establish a CFR cadence — regular 1:1 conversations tied to goals
  • Share OKR progress visibly with the wider team, not just leadership

90 Days

  • Run your first full OKR grading and retro at quarter close
  • Present a business review connecting engineering outcomes to company goals
  • Stand up an innovation accounting view for one active initiative
  • Recalibrate: if every OKR scored 1.0, raise the bar next quarter
🔄

Rituals That Keep Goals Honest

OKRs decay into wallpaper without a cadence that forces the team to look at them

Weekly

OKR check-in

15 minutes: confidence level per Key Result — on track, at risk, or off track — and what's blocking it.

Bi-weekly

CFR conversation

A real 1:1 tied to goals — not a status update, a conversation about what's working and what needs to change.

Sprint

Build-Measure-Learn retro

What did we learn this sprint, not just what did we ship? Update or kill hypotheses based on the data.

Monthly

Metrics audit

Review the dashboard for vanity metrics that crept back in. Cut anything that hasn't changed a decision in 90 days.

Quarterly

OKR grading & next-quarter planning

Score every Key Result 0.0–1.0. Discuss the score honestly, then set the next quarter's OKRs with that context.

Annually

Company-wide alignment session

Cascade top-level company Objectives down so every team can draw a straight line from their work to the business.

Key Takeaways

Five principles to carry forward

01

Objectives are the what and why; Key Results are how you'll know. Without both, you have a wish list, not a goal.

02

Committed goals should hit 100%. Aspirational goals landing around 70% is a sign you calibrated the ambition correctly — not that the team fell short.

03

Engineering exists to produce business outcomes, not just to ship code. Every roadmap item should trace to a Key Result, and every Key Result to a company Objective.

04

The Build-Measure-Learn loop turns assumptions into validated learning fast. Build the smallest thing that tests your riskiest assumption before you build the rest.

05

Vanity metrics feel good and change nothing. Actionable metrics are unglamorous and drive real decisions. Choose the dashboard accordingly.
