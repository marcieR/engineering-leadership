Session 7: Building a Roadmap

A roadmap isn't a promise of dates — it's a sequenced argument for why this work, in this order, gets you to the outcomes you committed to in your OKRs. The job is to be honest about confidence, not to look certain.

Field Heuristics for Roadmapping

Rules of thumb worth internalizing before you sequence a single ticket

3

Confidence horizons every roadmap needs — precise near-term, directional mid-term, thematic long-term

2×

A reasonable padding factor once an estimate crosses a team boundary or an unowned dependency

1

The number of Key Results every roadmap item should trace back to — if it's zero, ask why it's on the roadmap

🎭

The Core Problem: Date-Certainty Theater

Most roadmaps fail not because the work was hard, but because the roadmap lied about how well it was understood

A roadmap with a hard date on every row looks impressive in a slide and falls apart on contact with reality. Work you haven't scoped yet doesn't get more knowable by assigning it a quarter. The fix isn't fewer dates — it's being explicit about which items are backed by real estimation and sequencing work, and which are still a bet.

A roadmap's job is to communicate confidence honestly — not to look finished.

The Now / Next / Later Framework

Three horizons, three levels of detail, three levels of confidence

🧭

The Three Horizons

Match the precision of your roadmap to how well the work is actually understood

1

Now — this quarter, high confidence

Scoped, estimated, sequenced, and dependency-checked. This is the only tier where a sprint number or a date belongs.

2

Next — one to two quarters out, directional

The problem is well understood, the solution isn't fully designed yet. T-shirt sized, not story-pointed. Order can still shift.

3

Later — thematic, low confidence

A direction, not a plan. Exists to signal intent and gather feedback — not to be held to, and not to be estimated yet.

🗣️

Confidence Language

The words you use with stakeholders should match the horizon the work actually lives in

Committed

Scoped, estimated, and sequenced this sprint or quarter. You are accountable to this date — treat a miss as worth a retro.

Targeting

Real intent, real estimate, but a dependency or unknown could still move it. Say "targeting Q3," not "shipping Q3."

Exploring

Actively being scoped or prototyped. No estimate exists yet because the problem isn't fully understood.

Someday

On the radar, not resourced. Naming it prevents it from being silently forgotten, without pretending it's planned.

From OKRs to Roadmap

A roadmap is what your OKRs look like once you sequence them

🔗

The Chain of Custody for Every Roadmap Item

If you can't trace a row on your roadmap back to a business Objective, it doesn't belong there yet

Objective

  • The business result you're chasing this quarter or year
  • Set once, referenced constantly
  • Comes from Session 6's OKR work, not invented on the roadmap

Key Result

  • The measurable signal that the Objective is being achieved
  • Every roadmap initiative should move at least one of these
  • If an item can't be tied to one, question whether it's actually a priority

Roadmap Initiative

  • The actual body of work — scoped, estimated, and sequenced
  • Lives in Now, Next, or Later depending on how well it's understood
  • Carries a confidence label so stakeholders know how solid it is
🏚️

Orphaned Work vs. Work With a Home

The fastest way to bloat a roadmap is to let items onto it without a Key Result attached

Orphaned Work

  • Added because a stakeholder asked loudly, not because it moves a metric
  • Justified after the fact with a Key Result invented to fit it
  • Survives roadmap reviews by nobody asking "why is this here?"
  • Crowds out capacity that committed OKR work actually needs

Work With a Home

  • Exists because it's the clearest path to a specific Key Result
  • Can be cut in one sentence: "this moves KR2 from 40% to 60%"
  • Gets prioritized against other KR-linked work on its merits
  • Makes trade-off conversations concrete instead of political
Estimating & Sequencing Honestly

Order the work to retire risk early — not to satisfy whoever asked most recently

⚖️

Reactive vs. Risk-First Sequencing

The order you do work in matters as much as the estimates you attach to it

Reactive Sequencing

  • Whoever escalated most recently jumps the queue
  • Dependencies get discovered mid-sprint, not before
  • The riskiest, least-understood item is scheduled last "to be safe"
  • Surprises show up in week 10 of a 12-week initiative

Risk-First Sequencing

  • Order is set by dependency graph and confidence, not by volume of asks
  • Cross-team dependencies are flagged and sequenced before they block anyone
  • The riskiest, least-understood item is tackled first, while there's still time to react
  • Surprises show up in week 1, when they're still cheap to absorb

Sequence to retire your biggest unknown first — not to please whoever asked last.

Putting It Into Practice

What engineering managers and directors can do this quarter

🗓

30 / 60 / 90 Day Leader Actions

A phased plan for turning your OKRs into a roadmap people actually trust

30 Days

  • Sort the current roadmap into Now / Next / Later and relabel confidence honestly
  • Trace every "Now" item back to a Key Result — cut or park anything orphaned
  • Run the Estimation & Sequencing tool against your top 10 initiatives
  • Flag the single riskiest, least-understood dependency on the roadmap

60 Days

  • Resequence "Now" work so the riskiest item goes first, not last
  • Adopt confidence language ("targeting," "exploring") in every stakeholder update
  • Start tracking estimate variance to calibrate future sizing
  • Hold a cross-team dependency review before committing next quarter's "Now"

90 Days

  • Re-sequence "Next" into "Now" as a quarterly ritual, not an ad hoc scramble
  • Review variance data and adjust the 2× padding rule per team or dependency type
  • Present the roadmap alongside OKR progress in one leadership review
  • Retire or re-scope anything that's sat in "Later" for two quarters unchanged
🔄

Rituals That Keep a Roadmap Honest

A roadmap is a living document — treat it like one or watch it calcify into fiction

Weekly

Backlog refinement

Scope and estimate the next slice of "Now" work before it's needed, not the sprint it starts.

Sprint

Sequencing check

Re-confirm dependency order is still correct — teams and priorities shift underneath a roadmap constantly.

Sprint

Estimate variance review

Compare actual days to estimated days. Feed the delta back into how the team sizes similar work next time.

Monthly

Cross-team dependency sync

Surface anything in "Next" that depends on another team before it becomes a "Now" blocker.

Quarterly

Horizon shift

Move "Next" into "Now" and "Later" into "Next," re-scoping and re-estimating as confidence improves.

Quarterly

Roadmap-to-OKR audit

Check every item still traces to a live Key Result. Cut what doesn't, no matter how far along it is.

💡

Key Takeaways

Five principles to carry forward

01

A roadmap's job is to communicate confidence honestly, not to look finished. Match the precision of each item to how well it's actually understood.

02

Now / Next / Later isn't a formatting choice — it's a discipline for refusing to put a hard date on work you haven't scoped yet.

03

Every roadmap item should trace to a Key Result. If it can't, it's either orphaned work or a sign your OKRs are incomplete.

04

Sequence to retire your biggest unknown first. The riskiest item belongs at the front of the queue, not the back.

05

Estimates are a hypothesis, not a commitment, until dependencies are checked. Track variance and let it calibrate the next estimate.
