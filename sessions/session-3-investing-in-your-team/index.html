Fixed Mindset vs. Growth Mindset

Your brain actually changes through use — abilities can be developed

A Growth Mindset is about believing people can expand their abilities. The more you learn, the more your brain gets better at learning new things — it builds more neuron connections through use.

Fixed Mindset

  • Believes intelligence and ability are fixed traits
  • Avoids challenges to protect self-image
  • Sees effort as pointless or a sign of weakness
  • Ignores feedback
  • Threatened by others' success

Growth Mindset

  • Believes abilities can be developed
  • Embraces challenges as opportunities
  • Views effort as the path to mastery
  • Learns from criticism
  • Finds lessons and inspiration in others' success

Key principle

High standards + a nurturing environment = the conditions for growth.

⚠️

Common Misunderstandings

Four ways leaders accidentally undermine growth mindset

1

Taking what you like about having an open mind and assuming you have a growth mindset — they aren't the same.

2

Praising the outcome is praising an innate ability — instead, praise the process.

3

The "effort praise" pitfall — was the learner actually trying hard, or just going through the motions?

4

Telling someone "you can do anything" without providing structure, feedback, and strategy doesn't create growth.

🛠

Practical Techniques

Tools for recognizing and working past fixed mindset triggers

Giving your Fixed Mindset a name

Everyone has a fixed mindset in trigger situations — the inner voice that says "you aren't smart enough" or "you shouldn't keep trying."

Name that voice (e.g., "That's just Paul talking"). When it shows up, recognize it and work past the trigger deliberately.

Having a specific plan for doing hard things

The most common fixed mindset trigger is doing something important but not urgent. We defer — not because we forgot, but because the fixed mindset says "you're lazy."

Instead of willpower, use strategy: "Tomorrow when I'm drinking coffee, I will make that call." This is the growth mindset in action — employing a system to empower yourself.

Passion and Perseverance for Long-Term Goals

Engineering leadership is a long game

Grit ≠ Intensity

Grit ≠ Working late

Grit ≠ Hustle

Grit = Stamina

  • → Staying committed through multi-year transformations
  • → Holding the line on strategy amid shifting priorities
  • → Building durable systems, not short-term wins
  • → Leading through uncertainty, scrutiny, and incomplete information

Engineering leadership is a long game.

⚙️

The Four Drivers of Grit

What sustains perseverance at the individual and team level

1

Purpose

Teams persist when goals matter.

2

Deliberate Practice

Grit grows through structured learning.

3

Hope

Hope is believing effort leads somewhere.

4

Belonging

People persist when they feel part of something stable.

Talent vs. Grit

Duckworth's Achievement Formula

Effort counts twice

Skill = Talent × Effort

Achievement = Skill × Effort

Effort counts twice.

Engineering translation:

  • → Strategy matters
  • → But relentless alignment, reinforcement, and iteration determine success
🏗

Building a Gritty Organization

Leaders who compound outcomes over time vs. leaders who react

Grit is not "never failing." It looks like continuous growth, learning from failure, and staying aligned to enduring priorities.

  • ✓ Set multi-year technical direction
  • ✓ Reinforce priorities through governance and operating rhythms
  • ✓ Recognize long-term foundational work
  • ✓ Promote leaders who finish, not just start
  • ✓ Measure endurance, not intensity

Grit is what separates leaders who react from leaders who compound outcomes over time.
