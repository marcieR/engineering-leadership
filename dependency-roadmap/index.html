Dependency Roadmap Framework

A structured approach to identify, map, and manage cross-team dependencies that impact your roadmap. Use this framework to surface blockers early, align stakeholders, and ensure smooth execution across organizational boundaries.

How to Use This Framework

  1. 1 Identify Dependencies: List all initiatives that require input, resources, or deliverables from other teams
  2. 2 Map Relationships: Define what you need, who owns it, and when you need it
  3. 3 Assess Risk: Evaluate the likelihood of delays and impact on your roadmap
  4. 4 Create Mitigation Plans: Develop strategies to reduce risk and maintain momentum
  5. 5 Track & Communicate: Regular check-ins to ensure dependencies stay on track

Types of Dependencies

🔧 Technical Dependencies

APIs, infrastructure, platforms, databases, or technical services from other teams

Example: Need authentication API from Platform team

👥 Resource Dependencies

Shared engineers, designers, or other personnel needed from other teams

Example: Need 20% of backend engineer's time for integration

📄 Content/Data Dependencies

Documentation, data sets, content, or information from other teams

Example: Need product specs from Product team

✅ Approval/Decision Dependencies

Decisions, sign-offs, or approvals needed before proceeding

Example: Need security review approval before launch

🔄 Sequencing Dependencies

Work that must be completed by others before you can start

Example: Can't build UI until design system is complete

🤝 Cross-functional Dependencies

Collaboration needed with legal, finance, marketing, or other non-engineering teams

Example: Need marketing campaign ready for product launch

Best Practices for Managing Dependencies

✓

Surface Early & Often

Identify dependencies during planning, not during execution. The earlier you know, the more options you have.

✓

Be Specific, Not Vague

"Need backend support" is vague. "Need user profile API endpoints with pagination support" is specific.

✓

Build Relationships, Not Tickets

Dependencies are human relationships. Regular 1:1s with dependent team leads matter more than perfect tracking systems.

✓

Add Buffer Time

If a team says they'll deliver by March 1, plan as if it's March 15. Dependencies rarely arrive early.

✓

Make Trade-offs Visible

When dependencies slip, make the impact clear to stakeholders. "If API is delayed 2 weeks, launch moves to Q2."

✓

Have a Plan B

For critical dependencies, always have a contingency. Mocks, temporary workarounds, or reduced scope can keep you moving.

✓

Update Status Weekly

Dependencies change. What was green last week might be red this week. Regular check-ins prevent surprises.

✓

Escalate Early

If a dependency is at risk, don't wait. Escalate to your leadership and the dependent team's leadership immediately.

⚠️ Warning Signs Your Dependencies Are At Risk

  • • The dependent team hasn't confirmed your request in writing
  • • You're hearing "we'll try" instead of "we're committed"
  • • The dependent team's roadmap changed and your work isn't on it
  • • You haven't talked to them in 2+ weeks
  • • Status is "in progress" for 3+ weeks with no visible progress
  • • The owner changed and new owner is ramping up
  • • Dates are vague ("sometime in Q2") instead of specific
  • • You're the one doing all the following up

Dependencies managed well = roadmaps delivered on time
