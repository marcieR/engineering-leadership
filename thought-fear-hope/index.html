The Art of Hard Conversations

1:1 Framework

Hard conversations are where trust is built or broken. This framework helps you prepare for and navigate difficult 1:1 discussions with clarity, empathy, and courage. Whether it's performance feedback, conflict resolution, or delivering bad news, this structure ensures you show up as your best self.

Step 1: Pre-Conversation Preparation

Complete this section before the conversation

What is this conversation about?

Be specific. What behavior, situation, or issue needs to be addressed?

Why does this conversation need to happen now?

What's the urgency or impact if you don't address this?

What am I feeling about this?

Name your emotions: Anxious? Frustrated? Disappointed? Angry? Acknowledging this helps you show up with composure.

What assumptions am I making?

What story have I told myself? What might I be wrong about? Stay curious.

What's my desired outcome?

What would success look like after this conversation? What do you want to be different?

Step 2: The Conversation Structure

Follow this flow during the actual conversation

1

Set the Context (2-3 minutes)

Start by explaining why you're having this conversation. Be direct and clear about the topic.

Example Opening:

"I wanted to talk with you today about [specific situation]. This is important because [impact]. I want to understand your perspective and work together on a path forward."

2

Share Your Perspective (5-7 minutes)

Use specific examples and observations. Focus on behavior and impact, not character or intent.

The SBI Model (Situation-Behavior-Impact):

  • Situation: "In last week's sprint review..."
  • Behavior: "When you interrupted the designer three times..."
  • Impact: "It made them shut down and we lost valuable insights."

3

Listen to Their Perspective (10-15 minutes)

This is the most important part. Ask open questions. Listen without defending or interrupting.

Powerful Questions to Ask:

  • • "Help me understand your perspective on this."
  • • "What was going on for you in that moment?"
  • • "What am I missing or getting wrong?"
  • • "How do you see the situation?"

4

Co-Create Solutions (10 minutes)

Collaborative Questions:

  • • "What do you think we should do about this?"
  • • "How can I support you in making this change?"

Key Principles for Hard Conversations

✓

Assume Positive Intent

Most people aren't trying to cause harm. Start from curiosity, not judgment.

✓

Focus on Behavior, Not Character

"You interrupted three times" vs. "You're disrespectful."

✓

Be Specific, Not Vague

Use concrete examples with dates, times, and observable actions.

✓

Name the Emotion

"I felt frustrated" is more powerful than avoiding your feelings.

✓

Listen More Than You Talk

Aim for 60/40 - they should talk more than you do.

✓

Stay Curious

Ask "What am I missing?" instead of defending your position.

✓

Don't Delay the Hard Part

Get to the point within the first 3 minutes. The "sandwich method" erodes trust.

✓

Follow Through

The conversation doesn't end when you leave the room. Check in on commitments.

Common Hard Conversation Scenarios

Performance Issues

Missed deadlines, quality concerns, or work not meeting standards

Interpersonal Conflict

Team friction, communication breakdowns, or personality clashes

Behavioral Concerns

Unprofessional conduct, tone issues, or disruptive behavior

Delivering Bad News

No promotion, project cancellation, role change, or team restructure

Career Development Disconnect

When someone's aspirations don't match their performance or organizational needs

Remember This

Hard conversations don't get easier by waiting. They get harder. The cost of avoiding them compounds over time - trust erodes, resentment builds, and small issues become big problems.

The quality of your relationships is determined by your willingness to have hard conversations. People respect leaders who are direct, kind, and honest - even when the message is difficult.

You don't have to be perfect. You just have to be brave enough to start the conversation and humble enough to listen. The courage to be uncomfortable is the foundation of trust.
