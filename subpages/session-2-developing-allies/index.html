Segment 2 — Win-Win Leverage

Further Reading: The First 90 Days by Michael Watkins

Win-Win leverage is the ability to advance your agenda by helping others achieve their own and increasing your influence in the process.

🔗

What Win-Win Leverage Means in Practice

Trade value instead of pushing change through authority

Win-win leverage exists at the intersection of:

Your mandate

What you are accountable for

Others' priorities

What peers and stakeholders are measured on

Organizational constraints

Budget, time, risk, politics

🎚

The Three Levers of Win-Win Influence

Practical tools for building influence without authority

1

Leverage Through Early Wins

Early wins are not just about delivery — they are currency.

  • ✓ Select wins that matter to others, not just your function
  • ✓ Publicly credit cross-functional partners
  • ✓ Use early wins to earn future flexibility
2

Leverage Through Problem Framing

Whoever frames the problem controls the conversation.

Weak framing

"Engineering needs two more teams."

Win-win framing

"To hit the revenue milestone without increasing outage risk, we need to rebalance capacity between feature work and platform stability."

Reframe if your message includes:

"Sorry…" "We'll try…" "If it's okay…" "Because of X, we couldn't…" "Mistakes were made…"

Weak framing tends to:

  • → Lead with emotion (apology, defensiveness)
  • → Hide ownership
  • → Ask for permission instead of setting direction
  • → Frame work as pain instead of value
  • → Focus on why things failed instead of how they succeed next time
3

Leverage Through Dependency Mapping

Influence increases when you understand the full dependency graph:

  • → Who depends on you
  • → Who you depend on
  • → Where value can be exchanged

Formal dependencies

Roadmaps, approvals, sign-offs

Informal dependencies

Reputation, trust, institutional knowledge

This enables intentional reciprocity, not transactional bartering.
