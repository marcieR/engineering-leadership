Engineering Leadership Training

Session 1: Intro to Engineering Management

Becoming an Engineering Manager is a significant career inflection point. Rather than being the expert in a particular language, framework, or architecture pattern, you must shift your thinking and focus on achieving goals by building and leading a healthy team. Your new performance metrics include the career growth of your team members, roadmapping, and healthy delivery outcomes.

Segment 1: Balance

Further Reading: Dichotomy of Leadership by Jocko Willink

Dichotomy of Leadership

Leadership requires constant balance between opposing forces

Apply too much pressure / Applying not enough

Team feels like there is too much to do  /  Team slows down without a vision

No plan / Too much planning

Team doesn't know how to execute  /  Team is reluctant to execute without approval

Standards vs. Flexibility

Must hold the line with standards but notice when flexibility can be had.

Close but not too close

Leaders must be close with their people but not so close that it becomes an impediment to delivering feedback.

Ownership without over-control

Leaders must have ownership but not so much that the team only executes at the instruction of the boss.

Balance must be evaluated and modulated continuously

Example: if a team member falls behind, they may need more direct management. When they start performing to the standard, it's time to back off.

Doing the small things well

Increases trust — and when it matters, pushing back is well received.

Solid cross-functional relationships

Treat other departments with mutual respect and trust.

Humble enough to accept feedback

Must also be ready to hold the line when accepting feedback risks the larger mission. It's better to not execute a bad plan than to carry it out to the detriment of the team.

Focused but detached

Don't get bogged down by the details. Step out of the smaller details and see the bigger picture.

Leadership Qualities

Calm under pressure

Thinking clearly and making effective decisions in challenging situations.

Competent

Continuously building expertise and credibility with your team.

"Working yourself out of a job"

Building the team's capability so they can operate independently.

Navigating leadership and followership

The dichotomy every leader must strike — ready to lead and ready to follow.

Taking ownership of mistakes — no blame culture

We win as a team and fail as a team.

Four Principles of Extreme Ownership

1

Cover and move

Teamwork and mutual support — breakdown silos, win as a team, fail as a team.

2

Complexity breeds chaos

Instructions must be clear and concise.

3

Prioritize and execute

Clearly communicate priorities.

4

Decentralize command

Tell the team what to do AND why they are doing it.

Performance Management

Defining Performance

Outcomes + behaviors

Avoiding Bias

Watch for recency bias and halo effects — our brain's tendency to assume people skilled at one thing are skilled at everything.

Document Continuously

Don't rely on memory at review time — document performance observations regularly.

Tough Conversations

Navigate difficult performance discussions compassionately and directly.

Fair Promotions

Ground promotion decisions in evidence, not recency or personal affinity.

Feedback Models

Feedback types: reinforcing vs. constructive

SBI Framework

SSituation — the context in which the behavior occurred
BBehavior — the specific observable action
IImpact — the effect it had on the team/project

COIN Framework

CContext — in what situation did the events take place
OObservation — objective facts, not perceptions
IImpact — how it affected people, team, performance
NNext Steps — what can be done to improve going forward

Principles for effective feedback

  • Ensure feedback is actionable, kind, and specific
  • Build a feedback culture: model it, make it frequent, normalize it
  • Receive feedback without defensiveness

Overcoming Adversity with Cross-Functional Partners

The "Five Whys of Objection" Method

When someone pushes back, diagnose the real reason before responding:

1

They don't understand

2

They don't agree

3

They don't care

4

They don't believe it will work

5

They feel threatened or overwhelmed

You tailor your influence strategy based on which "why" you're solving.

The 3 C's: Clarity → Constraints → Collaboration

1. Clarity

Explain exactly what you want, why, and by when. Ambiguity kills influence.

2. Constraints

Show how your proposal works within reality: engineering capacity, design timeline, product priorities, customer needs.

3. Collaboration

Invite partnership, not obedience. People respond better to "let's solve this together" than "please do this."

Overcoming Adversity with Executive Comms

The Stakeholder Mapping + Influence Strategy

A go-to executive-level approach.

Step 1: Map stakeholders by:

  • Power: decision-making authority
  • Interest: how much they care

Step 2: Tailor your approach:

  • High power, high interest → co-create the plan
  • High power, low interest → speak in business outcomes
  • Low power, high interest → use as advocates
  • Low power, low interest → inform

This ensures you're using energy where it matters.
